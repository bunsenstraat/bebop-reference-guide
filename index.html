Bebop Dominant Scale — C D E F G A B♭ B♮ C
1
Which chord?
On a dominant 7th: reckon from the root (C7 → C bebop dom). On a minor 7th: reckon from its related dominant root (G- → use C bebop dom).
dominantminor ii
2
Rhythmic placement
In pure form: starts on a downbeat, starts on a chord tone (1, 3, 5, b7). This keeps all chord tones falling on beats.
3
Direction
The descending form is most common. Practice both. Line may ascend or descend in scalar fashion and return the same way.
4
The extra half step
The chromatic pass sits between b7 and the octave. If continuing: keep the half step. If ending a phrase: omit it (use whole step instead).
5
Starting on the 3rd
From the 3rd, you may descend chromatically to the 6th, or ascend then descend chromatically from the 3rd.
6
Phrase endings
Most phrases end on the upbeat of beats 1 or 3. Two common ending shapes: resolve down by half step, or resolve via b7 chromatic.
Bebop Major Scale — C D E F G G♯ A B C
1
Formula
Spelled 1-2-3-4-5-#5-6-7-8. Used over any major type chord. Chord tones for this scale are 1, 3, 5, and 6 (not 7).
major
2
Starting on the 9th
Descend chromatically from the 9th to the major 7th, then observe the basic rule from there.
3
Starting on the maj 7th
Descend chromatically from the major 7th to the 5th of the chord.
Same variety tricks apply
Start off beat 1 · vary the starting note · balance ascending/descending · bury the scale in a line · use turns on any chord tone · enclosure works on 1, 3, 5, 6.
Half-Diminished (ø) Chord
A
As ii (minor 7th function)
Treat the ø chord as a minor ii and apply all dominant bebop rules. E.g. Gø → C7: use C dominant bebop over both.
B
As VII in ii–V–I chain
In a G- C7 Eø A7 D- sequence: treat the ø as the ii of its related V. Eø is ii of A7 → use A dominant bebop over Eø.
When you start on a non-chord tone (or a chord tone falls on an upbeat), you need one of these moves to get a chord tone back on a downbeat as soon as possible.
Options when starting on a non-chord tone
1
Omit the extra half step
Use the scale without the chromatic passing tone. Proceed until a chord tone lands on a downbeat naturally.
2
Omit until b7, then restore
Skip the half step until you reach the b7. Once there, the normal half-step rule resumes.
3
Quarter-note the non-chord tone
Make the non-chord tone a quarter note so the following chord tone falls correctly on the next downbeat.
4
Insert chromatic approach
Insert a half step before the first chord tone you reach to restore metric balance.
5
Syncopate the chord tone
Tie the chord tone across the barline / into the downbeat (anticipation / syncopation). Classic bebop rhythmic feel.
6
From b2: approach tonic below
From the b2, approach the tonic from a half step below (i.e. via the leading tone).
7
From b3: approach 3rd above
From the b3, approach the 3rd from a half step above.
8
From #4: descend to 3rd
From the #4, descend chromatically to the 3rd.
9
From b6: approach 5th or b7
From the b6, approach the 5th from a half step below, or ascend chromatically to the b7.
11
The master rule
Move by step, half step, or skip until a chord tone occurs on a downbeat. All preceding options are implementations of this rule.
Triplets
Normal flow
Make the middle note of the triplet the chord tone (exception: going to the tonic — start on the maj 7th).
Starting triplet on chord tone
Use any non-chord-tone rule: enclosure, quarter note, syncopation, or omit the half step.
String of triplets
The last triplet in the string determines which rule applies. Work backwards.
Ways to extend / vary the bebop line
1
Maj7 chord ascent at b7
Upon arrival on the b7, ascend along a major seventh chord (e.g. Bb△9) to extend or change direction.
extension
2
Diminished outline at 3/5/b7
Upon arrival on the 3rd, 5th, or b7th, proceed along the diminished chord containing that note. Usually sets up a modulation.
extension
3
Deflection at the 5th
When leaving the 5th, deflect (briefly leave scale direction) then resume descent with the 5th on a downbeat. Great for modal situations.
extension
4
Enclosure
Delay arrival of the root or 5th by inserting the note one half step above and one half step below it first (bracket the target). Works from 3rd or b7 origins too.
technique
5
Join to other bebop scales
End one scale and flow directly into the next chord's bebop scale via conjunct motion (half or whole step). Keep the line continuous.
6
Join to other scale types
Transition from the bebop line into diminished, whole-tone, or diminished/whole-tone for color and contrast.
7
Delays / rhythmic displacement
Use dotted rhythms or rests to delay the start of the scale, then proceed normally. Adds rhythmic surprise.
8
Double time
Double the rate of notes within the same harmonic space. Use extension techniques to fill the extra space.
Variety within a single chord
Start off beat 1
Begin the scale on beat 2, 3, an upbeat, etc. The downbeat rule shifts accordingly.
Vary the starting pitch
Not just root and chord tones — any note. Non-chord-tone starts use the appropriate rules.
Balance direction
Alternate ascending and descending passages. Avoid monotone scalar direction.
Bury the scale
Hide the scalar motion inside leaps and other gestures so it isn't obvious as a straight scale.
Turns
Use ornamental turns on any chord tone.
Spins
Scalar ascending figures (3–6 notes) that restart from the next chord tone. Chain them for continuous motion.
